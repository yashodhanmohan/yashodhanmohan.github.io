What you're looking at.

Every sine wave is a rotation seen from the side. Mark a point on a circle, project its height onto a moving timeline, and you trace a sine. Each row above does this for one source: the circle's radius is the amplitude, how fast the dot rotates is the frequency, and the starting angle is the phase.

Add sines together and the bottom row becomes their sum. Stack the right harmonics and recognizable shapes appear — a square wave assembles itself from odd harmonics with amplitudes 4 / (πn); a sawtooth uses every harmonic with alternating signs; a triangle uses odd harmonics with a 1 / n² falloff. Fewer than infinite harmonics is why the corners ring; that's the Gibbs phenomenon.
